About the competition

The Royal Institute of Philosophy holds an annual essay prize open to all. In 2025, the topic was 'Bodies'. The brief was deliberately open: any philosophical approach to the body, from metaphysics to ethics to political philosophy, was welcome. The prize draws entries from across the world.

The essay: On the Death of Embodiment

Co-written with Pauline Verchinine

The essay traces a shift in Western moral thinking: the transition from a framework in which the body is the expression of a rational, intentional soul, to one in which the body has become an independent moral unit, tracked, measured, and governed on its own terms. We call the first perspective the embodiment view, drawing on Thomas Aquinas. We call the second the disembodied framework.

The argument moves through three historical movements. First, consequentialism relocated moral judgment from interior intention to observable outcomes, which made the body the natural site of moral assessment. Second, once collective welfare became the aim of public institutions, techniques of administration began to act directly on bodies as the only thing that governance can reliably reach. Third, scientism reinforced this by lending quantitative descriptions of bodily states the authority of moral norms.

We then examine what we consider two apparent escapes from this framework: contemporary individualism, which attempts to reclaim the body from collective norms while still measuring success on the same external scale; and subjectivism, which elevates inner experience over the body but in doing so produces a new dualism. Both reproduce the grammar of the framework they resist.
