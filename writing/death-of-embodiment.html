Essay · 2025 · Royal Institute of Philosophy Essay Prize

On the Death of Embodiment

Grégoire Préchac & Pauline Verchinine
← Back to writings

Abstract

How does the redefinition of bodies reflect the ethical framework of Western societies? In this work we oppose two perspectives, the first one that we call the embodiment perspective, put forward by Thomas Aquinas which considers the body as the expression of the soul. Their conjunction is the individual, the moral entity. In this framework, a body is judged insofar as it is the expression of the intentionality and not for itself. The second view, the disembodied movement, shaped by consequentialism, utilitarianism and scientism corresponds to a redefinition of the body as an independent entity (or for a totalitarian approach, the soul becoming part of the body). This view undermines the intentionality of an act and brings about a collective moral scale. The body becomes almighty as it becomes a malleable and judgeable agent on a societal scale. This movement produced two rebellious children: individualism and subjectivism, both of which appearing as reactions while grounding their opposition in the same framework they are resisting.

Introduction

'Action follows Being'. In this short sentence of Thomas of Aquinas lies the whole framework that shaped the perspective of essence in Western philosophy until the modern time. The essence is the fundamental nature of a thing, what it is. In the Thomist perspective, the body is considered as the expression of the essence of the individual, the particular human being. It is not a separate entity but an interacting matter that embodies the particular essence. The embodiment, as we will discuss in this work, refers to the representation of the individual in a bodily form. We here mean that the body is a representational and interacting matter with the soul. It is the incarnation of the essence. The Thomist perspective uses the soul and it is therefore used in this work. However the embodiment can also refer to the representation of the individual by the interaction between the body and what we would call today, the mind.

In contemporary Western society the body has become a separate and definite entity. What was the external representation of the essence has become the judgeable entity, that from which we determine the moral nature of an action. It has developed its own scales; health, weight, gender and measures. These characteristics have become more prevalent and most importantly, self sufficient. They are not a means to an end, the individual, but are, on their own, the interacting agent in an ethical framework. In this view, if the body is a prevalent aspect of society by becoming its own entity, its value is now a collective value and not a particular one. By becoming bodies, we have witnessed the death of embodiment.

In this work, we will develop the evolution of the vision of body and the shift of the moral agent in Western society: the transition from an individual value to a collective value. This work will answer the following question: How does the redefinition of bodies reflect the ethical framework of western society? We will first develop the embodiment perspective deriving from Thomas Aquinas, then we will introduce the utilitarian perspective and consequentialism view and their impact on the ethical framework and introduce the redefinition of bodies as agents and the role of scientism in its characterization. Finally this work will go into two modern pseudo reactions; individualism and dualism and develop their entanglement into what we call the disembodied framework.

1. Embodiment as the fundamental moral structure

Thomas of Aquinas begins from a determinate metaphysical question: 'What is the essence of a thing?'. In De ente et essentia, he calls essence 'that which is signified by the definition' (Aquinas, 1968) and which explains why a being is what it is and not something else. Essence is not a psychological interiority but a principle of intelligibility: it gathers what belongs to a being as such, independently of contingent properties. In the Thomist tradition, 'human being' is defined as a rational animal (homo est animal rationale). From this perspective, taken from Aristotle and integrated into Christian metaphysics, follow the properties of its essence. Animal refers to a living, sentient body. It is a living being as we would describe today. It is rational as it is endowed with the capacity for intellectual knowledge of the universal and for free will ordered to the good. Human essence thus comprises, at once, body and soul. More precisely, Aquinas adopts the Aristotelian scheme of form and matter. Every corporeal substance is composed of matter, which is the principle of potentiality and multiplicity, and form, which is the principle of actuality and unity. In the human case, the rational soul (anima rationalis) is the substantial form of the human body. The soul does not stand beside the body as a second substance; it is the intrinsic principle that makes matter a living human being. Because this soul is rational, the human body is not just any living body but the body of a rational animal, it has specific properties. Essence, however understood in this universal sense, is always realized in an individual subject. Humanity names what is common to all humans, but it never exists except in a particular; this human being. The principle of individuation is matter under determinate dimensions: this portion of matter, in this place, with these quantitative limits. It is this body, situated here and now, that receives the rational soul.

The Thomist definition of the essence of the human being therefore has three tightly connected aspects. First, it determines what the human is by nature: a rational animal, endowed with intellect and will. Second, it affirms that this rational life exists only as the life of a body, since the rational soul is the substantial form of a corporeal substance. Third, it situates this essence in a singular subject, a person, individuated by this body in this world. The essence is in this case realized in the ensouled, individual body, the embodied individual.

Since the rational soul is the substantial form of the body, the body is not added from outside to a complete spiritual subject. It is an intrinsic component of the human substance itself. The human being as such is the composite of soul and body. From this, it follows that the body shares in the ontological dignity of the person: it is not a neutral surface on which a subject might appear, but a constitutive element of what the subject is. Furthermore, this dignity is holistic. The living human body is not an aggregate of parts but the organised whole that the soul informs. This means that its ontological value is not dependent on the sum of matter's dignity. It is by the integration of the parts to the soul that makes dignity emerge. This also means that there is no glorifying of the parts of the body in themselves. An offense to the body's integrity is only an offence in so far as it is an attack to the integrity of the individual, the embodied soul.

Within this framework, the properties of the body such as strength, health or beauty are not supreme values in themselves. Bodily qualities and appearances acquire their proper meaning only as signs and instruments of interior dispositions and virtues. The value that the properties the body would carry depends on their relation to the rational subject whose body they characterize. They are the realization of the soul. There is therefore a concordance between body and soul: the body expresses, in its bearing and in its capacities, the orientations of the soul. The problem that arises is the following: if the body reflects the virtues of the individual then one could judge the individual by its external properties which could be contrary to the ideas of Christian faith ('Stop judging by mere appearances, but instead judge correctly' John 7:24 (The Holy Bible, 2011, p.1180)). In other words, if the body is the expression of the soul then we can get information on the soul by judging the body and so make a correct judgment.

However this arises only if we consider a direct translation of the properties of the body to the properties of the soul. In this case there is no direct access because the rational part of human beings brings about another property of the individual: intentionality. What causes an action to be good or bad is not dependent on the success of the action towards a goal or its intrinsic goodness but on the intentionality of the individual. This resolves the conflict brought on by the relationship between soul and body. A 'correct' judgment is one where the will of the individual, the intentionality, is known. Other judgments are biased by what we can perceive and most importantly what we cannot, intentionality.

The action is the external act of the individual through the body. What determines the action is precisely being. This is the meaning of the sentence 'Action follows Being'. More precisely, in Thomism, there are both the actus interior voluntatis, the inner act of the will, and the actus exterior, the external deed carried out through the body. The action is then the expression of a particular essence and intention, and this essence is embodied across time through action.

Intentionality is the primary cause of action and consequentially the primary source of morality. This does not mean that the external act and its consequences are morally indifferent. They are indirectly moral. An external act has moral significance because it is the ordinary place where an intention is expressed and because it touches goods that universally belong to human life. Here Aquinas introduces the framework of natural law and universal goods. Human nature, as rational animal, is ordered toward certain basic goods: life, truth, community, justice, the search for God. Natural law is nothing other than the participation of rational creatures in the eternal law, that is, the way in which human reason recognises and formulates these goods as ends that ought to be pursued and protected. An action is good when the will intends these goods under their proper aspect and chooses means proportioned to them. It is wrong when the will turns away from them or subordinates them to lesser ends.

Virtues are precisely the stable dispositions by which intentionality is ordered to these universal goods. A virtue is a habit that inclines the will to choose rightly, to love what ought to be loved and to reject what ought to be rejected. In this perspective, the same external act can take on different moral meanings depending on the intention and on its relation to goodness.

This hierarchy of intentionality and external act also structures both interpersonal judgment and the role of social laws. One can and must take account of visible behaviour, especially when it harms or benefits others, but Thomist ethics preserves the idea that the ultimate measure lies in the interior relation of the person to the good. At the level of society, law nonetheless has to regulate external acts. It does so, not because external conformity is the ultimate criterion, but because the community has to protect the basic goods of human life and to orient, as far as possible, the intentions of its members. Human law selects certain acts, those that most directly affect common goods such as life, justice or peace, and forbids or commands them. Its authority, however, is derived. It presupposes that these acts are ordinarily expressions of certain intentions and that they can either foster or distort the formation of virtuous habits.

Thus even when society judges and sanctions at the level of visible acts, the Thomist framework does not abandon the primacy of intentionality and essence. The external act is taken into account because it is understood as the normal manifestation of a rational will ordered, or meant to be ordered, to universal goods grounded in human nature. The 'scale' of morality, how we judge an action as good or bad, whether in personal evaluation or in public law, refers back to what the human being is and to its proper fulfilment, not to aggregate outcomes or to bare acts considered in isolation from the interior subject who performs them.

The Thomist framework has brought up a comprehensive and extensive vision of the universal essence of human being that expresses itself in individuals and that comprises both body and soul. The body realizes the soul in matter and its actions are the reflection of the individual's intentionality. This view focuses the moral judgment of an action on intentionality as the result of an action is just an indicator to the virtue of the individual. Its societal implementation induces moral judgement on actions in so far as it can orient intentionality towards virtues. This is the embodiment perspective which defines two moral entities: first, the embodied being comprised of (1) the intentionality and (2) the expression in the body and secondly an external act which is the product of the first kind and is socially judged based on its role in orienting intentionality towards virtues.

2. Collective values and the independent moral agent

In this work, we call an agent a responsible moral entity. It is independent when its responsibility can be stated in terms of its own principles of action, rather than being read off from a surrounding calculus. In the Thomist framework, the independent agent is the person, the ensouled being whose interior act of will is ordered to the good and expressed through external action. The body is constitutive of the individual, but it is not a separate moral unit.

2.1 Consequentialism and the spectator standpoint

Consequentialism holds that the moral status of an action depends on the value of its outcomes. In its classical utilitarian form, the right action is the one that maximises overall good, usually understood as happiness, pleasure, or preference satisfaction (Bentham, 1789; Mill, 1863). This doctrine is often presented as austere, but the present argument is not its austerity. It is its point of view. Consequentialism invites moral assessment to adopt a stance that is, in principle, third-personal: what matters is what happens in the world, for all affected, when one act rather than another is performed. The evaluation is therefore naturally expressed as a comparison between possible states of affairs (Sinnott-Armstrong, 2003; Driver, 2009).1

Two structural effects follow. First, morality becomes commensurative. The goods at stake must be put on a common scale, so that outcomes can be ranked as better or worse. Utilitarian reasoning is not just the claim that consequences matter, which Thomism can accept. It is the claim that consequences exhaust the relevant moral facts and that they do so in a form fit for comparison and aggregation. The decisive question becomes: among the available acts, which produces the greatest net balance of value?

Consequentialism requires that what matters be rendered comparable. It therefore presupposes an epistemological operation: the construction of a moral scale. A scale here is not first a metaphysical discovery but a rule of measurement, a way of translating heterogeneous goods into a common currency so that actions and states of affairs can be ranked. In this sense, the scale is a human artefact, yet one that claims objectivity once instituted. It selects what counts as welfare, determines how increments are counted, and establishes a criterion of improvement. Moral reasoning becomes, structurally, the placement of acts and persons upon this measure.

Once such a measure is adopted, it also claims universality. It is not one scale for one agent and another for a second; it is a single scale applicable, in principle, to all participants in the society. This is decisive. The good is no longer articulated as the perfection of a particular rational nature realized in singular persons, but as a distributable magnitude whose units are the same across individuals. The moral question is thereby displaced: it is no longer primarily: what is fitting to this person as a rational animal ordered to objective goods?, but: where does this life, this body, this action, fall on the common metric that governs all?

Second, morality becomes publicly legible. Consequentialist evaluation does not require access to intention in of itself. It can include intention only as a causal variable, that is, as something relevant insofar as it tends to produce certain outcomes. The moral language that results is therefore naturally addressed to what can be observed and predicted: harms prevented, benefits produced, risks reduced, welfare increased. Consequentialists can evaluate expected consequences under uncertainty. The point is not that consequentialism condemns every failure, but that the normative currency is now external: expectation, probability, outcome, distribution.

This shift alters what can count as the moral unit in practice. Once the good is formulated as a pattern in the world that can be compared across options, the agent is naturally redescribed as the locus through which those patterns are produced. In public and institutional contexts, one cannot reliably inspect the inner act of will; one can, however, register external behaviour and its effects. From the spectator standpoint, the body is the visible site where actions occur and from which consequences radiate. Moral discourse therefore begins to attach responsibility to the causal profile that can be attributed and tracked, and this profile is, in ordinary life, inseparable from the body.

What is gained is a universal grammar for public evaluation. What is lost is the necessity of embodiment as the passage from interior apprehension of the good to external act as its expression. The moral system can operate, at least at the level of social judgment, without knowing the interior ordering of the person.

2.2 From the spectator to administration: why bodies become the site of moral governance

If the end goal of society is described as the maximisation of an epistemological scale that measures aggregate welfare, then the central practical problem becomes the status of the placements on that scale. Are they determinate or indeterminate? If they are determinate, if individuals can be reliably located on the metric and predicted as higher or lower contributors to aggregate welfare, then the rational strategy is selection: favouring, promoting, or reproducing those who rank higher, and discouraging, excluding, or preventing those who rank lower. One need not invoke an explicit doctrine of eugenics for the logic to be eugenic in structure: optimization by selection follows from a determinate metric pursued under aggregate aims.

If, however, the end goal is not determinate and the metric is taken to be real and obligatory but individuals are not stably classifiable, then the strategy shifts. The goal is no longer primarily to select but to modify. To achieve the end goal society needs to transform individuals so that the agents move upward on the scale. The scale remains; what changes is the object of action. Instead of being judged as the expression of an interior ordering to the good, individuals are approached as improvable systems whose measurable parameters must be corrected. The spectator standpoint becomes the administrative standpoint: a society committed to aggregate maximisation will naturally develop techniques that act upon what can be measured, trained, constrained, treated, and normalised, namely, bodies. Once the good is conceived as something that can be measured as a distribution across many lives, it becomes natural to ask how the distribution can be improved by coordinated interventions. The moral gaze becomes, increasingly, an administrative gaze.

The body enters with a new prominence, not because it has become more 'real' than the individual, but because it is what governance can act upon. The interior act of will does not present itself to public techniques of correction. Bodies do. They can be arranged in spaces, subjected to timetables, and made to conform to norms of performance. Foucault's analyses of discipline describe precisely this emergence of techniques that produce 'docile bodies', bodies that are both useful and obedient (Foucault, 1977). His further discussions of biopolitics extend the point: modern power does not only punish; it optimises life by managing the biological and social variables of populations (health, reproduction, morbidity, productivity). In that context, goodness is no longer only a set of end goals for individuals; it is that which the system targets.

Once welfare is conceived as an aggregate pattern and once improvement is conceived as an institutional project, the ethically relevant features of individuals will be those that can enter the variables of management. The body supplies those variables. It becomes the interface between moral discourse and practical intervention. This does not necessarily happen by a metaphysical claim of the body as the moral agent. It is enough that moral reasons are increasingly stated in terms that treat bodily states as the proper objects of correction: weight as risk, sleep as productivity, mood as function, sexuality as health, attention as performance. The body is thereby addressed as if it were the bearer of responsibility, because it is the bearer of the parameters that institutions can normalize.

At this point, the moral language of modernity begins to change its grammar. Where Thomism speaks the language of virtue and interior ordering to the good, this framework speaks the language of optimisation, prevention, and risk. The individual is not denied, but it becomes secondary. What can be evaluated, compared, and improved is what is visible and governable: bodies and their measurable effects.

2.3 Scientism and the authority of measurement

A third movement consolidates the previous two: the elevation of scientific description, especially quantitative description, into the privileged form of truth about human life. By scientism we do not mean a claim about the epistemological status of science, but the stronger tendency to treat scientific metrics as uniquely authoritative even in domains where evaluative and practical judgment cannot be reduced to measurement (Sorell, 1991; Haack, 2003).

Scientism stabilises the body's new moral centrality by conferring a special authority on measures of bodily normality and deviation. Within the described framework, where the body is treated as a set of variables that can be regulated, and collective welfare is the aim, scientific description appears to deliver the very language in which the good can be stated. 'Normal range', 'risk factor', 'evidence-based', 'statistically significant' functions not only as epistemic terms but as normative ones. The boundary between what is and what ought to be narrows, because what is measurable is taken to be what is most real, and what is most real is taken to be what must govern our choices.

This has two consequences. First, moral evaluation becomes increasingly inseparable from quantification. What cannot be measured is easily treated as mere sentiment or private preference; what can be measured readily becomes eligible for institutional attention and moral urgency. Second, norms acquire the appearance of inevitability. If the relevant standards are read off from epidemiology, psychology, or economics, then deviation becomes not merely difference but error: a failure to manage oneself according to the defined truth. The resulting structure is a closed circuit: external welfare is the end; bodily variables are the means; scientific metrics define which bodily variables matter and what counts as improvement.

Consider Léa, thirty four, accountant, she eats, sleeps and works, she even has friends. She is what you would call: a normal girl. At her annual check up she is weighed, measured, and classified. She is below the threshold. The consultation is short, the language is clean, and the conclusion is immediate: 'risk'. Her employer offers a rebate on her health insurance if she joins a program, logs her meals, follows a prescribed plan, and shows improvement over time. Léa's body becomes one dot among thousands, and her dot must travel. A year later she returns, having passed the threshold and expecting the rebate she was promised. The number has changed. But so did the program. During the year, the program was 'updated'. The thresholds were recalibrated as the population trend shifted. The mean now sits lower. She is one step too far. The conclusion is immediate: 'risk'.

The body fully occupies the practical place of the independent agent. It is tracked across time; it is credited and blamed; it is invited to justify itself in the language of its numbers. It is not Léa that is evaluated but the datapoint compared to the chosen scale.

The common effect of these three shifts is therefore not a precision of the properties of the body, but a restructuration of the role of body as agent and with this of what moral life is for public purposes. Morality becomes external and comparative; the primary object of concern becomes the body as a governable system of parameters; the authority of norms becomes the authority of measurement. In that sense, the body replaces the embodied individual as the operative unit of moral assessment. We do not cease to be individuals. We are treated, increasingly, as bodies occupying positions on external scales. We call this shift, the disembodied framework. By becoming bodies, we witness the death of embodiment.

3. Escaping the disembodied framework: contemporary individualism and subjectivism

From the disembodied framework grew two rebellious children: contemporary individualism and subjectivism. Both praise the courage to stand apart and to live a life that is one's own. The collective order appears as something to be rejected rather than inhabited. In Two Ages: a Literary Review, Kierkegaard insists on the single individual against the crowd, but also notes how easily the individual lets the public dictate what is admirable and contemptible (Kierkegaard, 1846). This suggests a pattern in which dissatisfaction with collective norms gives rise to reactions that derive their shape from what they oppose. Therefore both contemporary individualism and subjectivism could be described as reactionist, or pseudo-reactionist.

Contemporary individualism aims at giving back the moral responsibility to the individual. Subjectivism focuses on inner life and claims that the true self resides in subjective experience, regardless of the situation of the body. Both operate against the background of a framework in which consequences and observable actions have priority in moral evaluation. One tries to withdraw the body from the reach of this framework. The other tries to withdraw the mind.

3.1 Contemporary individualism: private interests on a public scale

The first attempt begins from the observation that collective norms address the subject at the level of the body. In response to this, rhetoric emerges which encourages subjects to reclaim their bodies. A good life is described as one in which one's body does not depend on the collective. In The Ethics of Authenticity, Charles Taylor develops how individuals are urged to be true to themselves against external pressures, while the very idea of a fulfilled life is still shaped by social understandings of what counts as choiceworthy (Taylor, 1992). In the bodily case, this dynamic appears as an effort to extract the body from collective norms while still taking the form of those norms as the starting point. The measure of success remains how the body stands in relation to a surrounding order.

The body is placed at the centre and a further question arises about the status of the individual who inhabits it. To say that the body should no longer depend on collective structures is not yet to say that the individual can exist morally by themselves. The conditions that define bodily independence have to be recognised in common if they are to guide action. In the same vein, MacIntyre's After Virtue: A Study in Moral Theory emphasises that what appear as free choices of individuals are in fact made within practices and institutions that supply the standards by which those choices are evaluated (MacIntyre, 1981). The criteria by which independence is recognised are still relational. A body counts as independent if it can maintain itself without particular kinds of support. None of these conditions can be described without reference to the social and economic arrangements from which they distinguish themselves. The moral standing of the individual therefore still depends on how their body is situated within a collective structure. There is no solitary scale on which the individual could be judged in abstraction from that structure.

The individual is told to make their body their own, and to take distance from collective norms. Yet the very possibility of such "distance" is measured within the shared evaluative space those norms provide. The body acquires a new value as something that should be kept free from certain constraints, and acting acquires a new aim in the effort to achieve that freedom. The ethical scale, however, remains external, since praise and blame still track visible positions relative to institutional ties. What presents itself as the pursuit of one's own interests therefore continues to consult a collective measure to determine what counts as a worthwhile interest, and what counts as success. The individual does not step outside the scale; they redescribe their life as a personal project while still locating its meaning on the public metric. What has been established is an independence of the body from certain forms of collective organisation, not an independence from the collectively defined scale by which independence is recognised.

3.2 Subjectivism: a return to dualism

The second attempt begins from the inner side of experience. The fact that the body has become the main bearer of external evaluation does not remove the sense that the self is also, and perhaps primarily, a matter of thought, feeling and intention. Philosophical accounts that distinguish mind and body give support to this sense. Descartes' Meditations describe the self as a thinking thing whose essence consists in thought and which can be clearly conceived apart from the body (Descartes, 1641). In such a view, the mind is an agent in its own right, capable of doubt, affirmation and will, even if all bodily attributes are stripped away. In a context where the body is regulated and measured from outside, this suggests that the mind of the individual can retain a kind of freedom as their own agent, even if the body remains caught in external systems. This turn to the mind preserves subjective intentions and experiences as an independent pole of agency.

If what truly matters about the self is the content of consciousness, then that content can change, proliferate and contradict itself without necessarily altering what is done with the body. Inner life becomes a space of fluid possibilities, no longer required to settle into stable patterns of action in order to count as real. The surrounding evaluative framework, which reads bodies through their consequences and actions, has no need to follow these inner shifts unless they result in visible differences.

If external norms are perceived as oppressive, and bodily conditions are seen as dominated by these norms, then inner experience can be taken as the last remaining place where the individual can affirm themselves. This becomes the thesis that authenticity consists above all in being faithful to one's own feelings and experiences, even when these cannot be realized externally. The result is a hierarchy in which subjective, internal and immaterial experience takes precedence over enactment. The body may remain constrained, but the mind can still claim superiority by virtue of its private sense of meaning. As a result, the mental domain obtains an agent status that no longer depends on its transition into visible action.

When the external evaluation of bodies and the internal elevation of mind coexist, a new dualism appears. Descartes separated res cogitans from res extensa, locating the self in thinking rather than in bodily extension (Descartes, 1641). In contrast, Merleau-Ponty's Phenomenology of Perception insists that subject and body are inseparable in lived, embodied perception and action (Merleau-Ponty, 1945). Here the body is addressed from outside as a unit of consequences to be managed, while the mind is treated from inside as a locus of intentions that can judge its own life without being bound by what the body does. Intentionality remains not required to go from representations of the mind to actions of the body. Two centres of agency occupy the same life, one answering to external criteria and the other to its own internal norms. This is a new dualism, in which embodiment as a transition from inner act to outer deed is no longer presupposed.

The coexistence of these two agents has consequences for the stability of the subject. In so far as the body is forced by external evaluation into particular patterns of behaviour, and the mind is free to set its own preferences and narratives, their interests can diverge. The inner agent may refuse the work, habits or relationships that the bodily agent must sustain in order to meet external demands, and the bodily agent may continue to comply long after inner assent has been withdrawn. This divergence can produce indecision, as neither agent is able to impose a coherent course of action, and stagnation, as projects are continuously revised without being carried through. This self division can lead to despair, such as Kierkegaard's account of a self that wills to be itself but cannot reconcile its different dimensions (Kierkegaard, 1849). Such a split can threaten the very unity of the individual. In more extreme cases, when inner narratives and bodily realities no longer intersect in a shared world, the individual risks a breakdown in which neither agent can recognise itself in the other. This forced cohabitation of mind and body without embodiment is therefore a pressure towards fragmentation that could become pathological, something akin to depersonalization-derealization.2

Conclusion

The redefinition of bodies in contemporary Western societies is the visible expression of an ethical reorientation. Whereas the Thomist framework treats the body as the realization of a rational soul where moral appraisal ultimately depends on the interior act of will ordered to the good, modern moral life increasingly operates through external, comparative, and publicly legible criteria. Once moral evaluation is organised from the spectator standpoint of consequences, goods must be rendered commensurable, distributed on a common scale, and assessed without privileged access to intention. The body then becomes the natural site of moral judgment, not because it is metaphysically identical with the person, but because it is what can be observed, measured, and governed.

This trajectory is intensified when the spectator standpoint becomes administrative. A society committed to maximising collective goods will tend to treat persons as systems to be optimised and corrected, and the body supplies the parameters through which such optimisation can be pursued. Scientism consolidates this transformation by granting quantitative description an authority that easily takes on normative force: what is measurable appears most real, and what is most real appears most obligatory. In this circuit, moral discourse shifts from virtue, formation, and the interior ordering of the will to the language of compliance, risk, normality, and performance. The operative moral unit becomes the body positioned on external scales, and the embodied person, the unity of interior intentionality and exterior action, becomes increasingly dispensable for the functioning of public moral order, what we call the disembodied framework.

Contemporary individualism and subjectivism could be interpreted as reactions to this framework, yet they largely reproduce its grammar. The individualism of the body seeks independence from collective norms but still measures success through externally defined relations of detachment; it remains bound to the very scale it resists. The subjectivism considers the inner domain as primary, but in doing so it reduces the interactions between the mind and the body. Together, mind and body generate a new dualism: a body addressed from outside as a bearer of outcomes and a mind addressed from inside as a private source of meaning. The result is the forced cohabitation of two partial agencies within a single life, a configuration that pressures unity and risks fragmentation.
